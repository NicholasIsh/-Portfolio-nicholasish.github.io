My experience continuing the development of my website:

My IxD Process during the development of my website:
My Goal: I want my website to impress users with its clean, elegant design and effectively communicate my skills as a web designer. I want users to be inspired by my work and motivated to contact me for design services or exploring more of my portfolio.

Information structure:
The website features a navigation bar at the top of each page for easy access. The home page introduces the creator and website. The blog page has a selection of blogs with a fixed navigation bar on the right for easy access, which remains visible while scrolling down the page. The design page displays a document in the centre left, detailing the website's design, with a similar navigation bar on the right. The profile page features a picture of the creator and information about their skills and interests, with a link to their portfolio page. The portfolio page has a similar layout to the design page, with a navigation bar on the right for easy navigation.

User Flow:
The website's home page is the starting point, with a top navigation bar to move to other pages. Blog, design, and portfolio pages have a similar layout, with a fixed navigation bar on the right side for easy access to specific sections. Users can scroll down to read more information on these pages. The profile page also has a scrollable layout and a button to access the portfolio page.

Reflection on this week's reading:
I found Hassenzahl and Tractinsky's paper "User Experience - A Research Agenda" to be a very insightful paper which focuses on the user experience. Hassenzahl and Tractinsky’s aim is to understand and improve user experience, emphasising the importance of understanding user needs, emotions, and behaviours. From the paper I have learnt the importance of considering the entire user experience, rather than just individual components. This means that as a website developer, I need to think about the user's experience from the moment they land on the website to the moment they leave. In developing my website I intend to keep the principles outlined in this paper in mind as they could be incredibly helpful and I can create a website that not only meets functional requirements but also provides a positive user experience. By considering the user's needs and emotions I can create a website that is not only functional but also enjoyable to use.

Where did I struggle?

While I did eventually overcome these difficulties, I recognize that I could have saved myself a lot of time and frustration by seeking out more resources and guidance on CSS formatting earlier in the process.

One thing I would definitely do differently is to rework the semantic markup of my site. While the overall layout and aesthetic of the site were successful, I know that there are improvements that can be made in terms of the underlying HTML structure. By focusing on improving the semantic structure of the site, I believe that I can not only improve its accessibility and search engine optimization, but also make it easier to maintain and update in the future.

Reflection on Graham et al. - 2015 - Towards a study of information geographies:
I found this reading to be very interesting and raises important questions about the distribution of internet access around the world as it highlights the divide between the global south and the north. This uneven distribution of internet access and information flows is shaped by a complex interplay of historical, political, economic, and cultural factors.

Reflection on the Digital Hegemonies reading:
Ballatore's 2017 paper on "Digital Hegemonies" provides a critical perspective on the global dominance of search engines and their impact on local communities. Ballatore argues that despite the global reach of search engines, their algorithms are shaped by local factors such as language, culture, and geography, leading to the formation of digital hegemonies that privilege certain regions and communities over others, namely the global north. This paper ties to the previous reading on Information geographies as it highlights the uneven distribution of digital resources and power. Both papers emphasise the importance of studying the spatial dimensions of digital inequality and the need for more nuanced approaches to understanding the complex ways in which technology and geography interact.

References:

Ballatore, A., Graham, M. and Sen, S., 2017. Digital hegemonies: the localness of search engine results. Annals of the American Association of Geographers, 107(5), pp.1194-1215.

Graham, M., De Sabbata, S. and Zook, M.A., 2015. Towards a study of information geographies:(im) mutable augmentations and a mapping of the geographies of information. Geo: Geography and environment, 2(1), pp.88-105.

Week 9

Reflection on the reading 'Digital inequalities in the age of artificial intelligence and big data' by Lutz(2019):
Lutz's 2019 paper on digital inequalities provides a review of the relationship between digital inequalities and its context in the world. Lutz emphasises the need for research to focus more on labour and big data-related questions, with a focus on the negative impacts that algorithmic decision making can have upon vulnerable population groups. Lutz highlights three main levels of digital divides which can be used to better understand digital iniquities. The author discusses access to digital technologies, digital skills and technology use, and outcomes of digital technology use, highlighting the need to integrate literature more strongly with critical algorithm studies and discussions on datafication, digital footprints, and information privacy. This reading serves as an important reminder of the continued existence of digital inequalities in an age of artificial intelligence and big data. Lutz calls for further research to address these issues and highlights the potential negative consequences of algorithmic decision-making for marginalised groups. It is a valuable contribution to the ongoing discourse on digital inequalities and their implications for society.

Reflection on “The Road to Technocolonialism”:
I found “The Road to Technocolonialism” to be very interesting as it raises significant concerns on the dominance of AI development being held by the US and China and how this situation could lead to a new form of colonialism where the global south is dependent on nations like the US for access to AI technology. Developing countries, lacking the infrastructure to develop their own AI systems, are likely to enter into agreements with the AI superpowers, trading access to technology for economic aid and political gains. This can lead to a situation where the US and China can use this as a tool to exert political and economic power onto developing nations. This is a terrifying possibility as these AI client states will be at the full mercy of these superpowers especially as technology progresses and AI technology becomes more prevalent in society. These superpowers could potentially cripple a nation by cutting off their access to essential technologies required in the modern era. This centralised control and ownership of technology by the AI superpowers will lead to a form of domination and the erosion of local autonomy. There is undoubtedly a need to address emerging technocolonialism. If left unchecked, developing nations will be faced with a choice between becoming a techno colony or a “New Dark Age" (Verdi, 2020).

Reference:
Giorgos Verdi, 2020, “The Road to Technocolonialism”, Institute for Internet & the Just Society, URL: https://www.internetjustsociety.org/the-road-to-techno-colonialism [accessed: 05/05/2023]

Reflection on Digital Inclusion:
The reading highlights the crucial issue of digital inclusion and digital exclusion in the increasingly digitised modern world. It raises the importance of equal access to information and communication technologies for individuals and groups and the lack of this in the 21st century. Africa is lagging behind the rest of the world in terms of internet access with only 39.3% of inhabitants having access to the internet. This is in contrast to America's 94.6% which clearly shows a digital divide between the continents and highlights the need for digital inclusion. The article also brings some light on how rural areas have much to offer in terms of uniqueness and diversity to the online market. However, the lack of access to the internet creates a barrier, preventing the recognition and appreciation of rural cultures. Digital inclusion should be a priority for all designers as only by promoting universal access and digital literacy can we create a more inclusive and equitable digital society.

Reflection on Tataki and Glynos - 2020 - Digital Divide Widens:
This reading highlights how the current digital divide has only been widened by the COVID-19 pandemic. This digital divide disproportionately affects vulnerable groups like students from low-income families as well as ethnic minorities.This divide was made larger through the transition to remote work as it is primarily available to high income individuals in occupations that do not require physical interaction. This leaves less privileged workers, who are more likely to face job insecurity, at a disadvantage. The economic challenges resulting from the pandemic also threaten jobs in low-paying sectors that require physical interaction with customers or close proximity to others. Tataki and Glynos(2020) do mention some short term solutions that have been implemented such as the UK government providing students with laptops however they also recognise that there is a need for more long-term solutions to close the digital divide and bring about equal access to digital infrastructure world wide. If we don’t address the digital divide soon we will see a world of increasing inequality where vulnerable groups are unable to fully access and utilise the internet putting them at a greater disadvantage to the upper classes. In the modern era, we should recognise the importance of internet access as a basic need as I believe that this will help us to work towards closing the digital divide and create a more inclusive society.
